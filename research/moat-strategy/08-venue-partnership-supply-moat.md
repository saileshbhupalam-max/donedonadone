## 1. The Supply-Side Moat in Marketplaces

### 1.1 Why Supply-Side Lock-In Matters More Than Demand-Side

In two-sided marketplaces, the supply side is almost always the "hard side" -- the constrained resource that determines whether the marketplace can function at all. Academic research on switching costs identifies three types that apply directly to supply-side lock-in: **procedural switching costs** (loss of time and effort to migrate), **financial switching costs** (loss of financially quantifiable resources like historical data and established revenue streams), and **relational switching costs** (psychological and emotional discomfort of abandoning a working partnership).

**Core thesis: In a marketplace where suppliers invest effort, data, and operational workflow into your platform, the cost of leaving compounds over time -- creating a moat that deepens automatically.**

#### Key evidence from major marketplaces:

**Airbnb (5M+ hosts, 8M+ active listings)**
- Airbnb's host referral program became "the single most efficient and effective growth lever for consumer supply, cost efficiently driving both the largest share of attributable supply and the highest quality supply" (Lenny Rachitsky, ex-Airbnb Supply Growth Lead)
- Hosts accumulate reviews over months and years. A host with 50+ reviews and Superhost status faces enormous switching costs -- moving to a competitor means losing that entire reputation
- Airbnb's 2026 strategy includes a Host Services Marketplace (hire cleaners, maintenance) that shifts Airbnb from booking intermediary to operational hub, deepening supply-side lock-in through ecosystem integration
- Critical mass threshold discovered internally: **300 listings with 100 reviewed listings** in a market triggers a growth inflection point
- The Airbnb Plus quality certification increased weekly booking rates by **6.8-7.6%**, translating to **$3,100+ in additional revenue per certified property** -- for a $149 inspection fee
- At a platform level, Plus certification increased revenues by **$80,000+ per year** for the average 2km zone containing Plus listings

**DoorDash (Supply-Side Tools)**
- DoorDash provides restaurants with a comprehensive Merchant Portal including Sales Reports (marketplace sales, DashPass orders, average ticket size, new vs. return customers), Operations Reports (order accuracy, cancellations, wait time, downtime, ratings, product mix), and Financial Reports (transactions, error charges, payouts)
- The Insights Hub includes an "Optimization Score" -- an estimate of a store's unrealized sales potential on DoorDash
- Introduced tiered commissions between **15% and 30%**, allowing restaurants to trade margin for greater reach, visibility, or delivery range
- Partnered with ItsaCheckmate and Nextbite for data aggregation, creating an ecosystem that becomes harder to leave

**Instacart (From Marketplace to Operating System)**
- Built integrations with retailers' core systems -- POS, catalog, loyalty programs, couponing -- making it "easier to launch new technology offerings for their stores"
- **Storefront Pro**: White-label e-commerce platform powering retailer-owned storefronts with Instacart technology
- **Store View**: AI-powered computer vision collecting real-time shelf inventory data
- **Caper Carts**: Smart carts with computer-vision auto-detection allowing checkout in 8 seconds
- **Retail Media Network**: One of the largest grocery commerce media networks, enabling brands to advertise on retailer websites
- Strategy: deeply integrate with partners' operations so the cost of switching becomes operational risk, not just financial cost

**Uber (Driver Lock-In Through Financial Infrastructure)**
- In new markets, subsidized drivers heavily -- "spending billions of investors' cash before cutting driver payments" to reach supply density
- Internal documents reveal Uber aimed to understand "the net fare/hour at which supply scales" and "make sure drivers don't end up making more than they need to stick around"
- **Uber Pro Card** (launched October 2022): branded business debit card + checking account embedded in the Uber app, enabling instant per-trip payment and in-app fund management
- Only **~4% of drivers remain on platform after one year** (2017 analysis), yet financial products (instant pay, vehicle financing, insurance) create lock-in for those who stay
- In Paris, initial subsidies of ~$4/hour were eventually cut to nearly zero; in mature markets, incentives dropped to $0.10/hour while platform revenue hit $23.40/hour

### 1.2 How OpenTable Created the Deepest Supply-Side Moat

OpenTable pioneered the "SaaS + marketplace" bundling model that is now considered the gold standard for supply-side lock-in.

**The OpenTable Playbook:**

1. **Started with SaaS (1998)**: Sold Electronic Reservation Book (ERB) software to restaurants in San Francisco -- a computerized system replacing pen-and-paper reservation management
2. **Solved the cold-start problem**: The software was valuable as a "single-player mode" tool even without diners on the platform. Restaurants used it purely for operational management
3. **Aggregated supply density**: By selling SaaS to restaurants in one geography, OpenTable inadvertently aggregated the supply-side density needed to jumpstart its marketplace
4. **Layered in marketplace**: Once enough restaurants were using the software, OpenTable activated the demand side -- consumers booking through OpenTable's app/website
5. **Created system of record**: OpenTable became "the de facto system of record for many customers, which locks restaurants in to using the platform ensuring OpenTable captures each transaction"
6. **Revenue model**: Subscription fees (Basic/Core/Pro tiers) + per-reservation fees (~$1 per network booking) + installation fees + advertising revenue

**Results:**
- 60,000+ restaurants on platform
- Seats 1 billion+ diners annually
- IPO valuation: ~$626 million (2009)
- Acquired by Booking Holdings for **$2.6 billion** (2014)
- Total VC funding: just $48 million across three rounds

**Key insight for donedonadone:** OpenTable's genius was making the SaaS product valuable independent of the marketplace. Restaurants would stay even if they could get bookings elsewhere, because the operational tools were indispensable. This is the "Venue Operating System" strategy donedonadone must pursue.

### 1.3 How ClassPass Created Studio Dependency

ClassPass operates with a network of approximately **30,000 partner studios and wellness centers** as of 2025. Its model demonstrates how volume aggregation creates supply-side dependency.

**The Dependency Mechanism:**
- **Monetizing excess capacity**: ClassPass positions itself as filling otherwise-empty class slots, not replacing existing members. "Most businesses use ClassPass to drive incremental revenue... the goal is to monetize capacity that would have otherwise gone unused"
- **Revenue impact**: Studios using ClassPass see a **28% increase in incremental revenue** after just 6 months on the platform (as of January 2025)
- **Reservation volume**: Studios typically see a **30% increase in reservation volume** and a **15-20% increase in revenue** when using ClassPass SmartTools
- **Retention**: The ClassPass retention rate for businesses earning >$50/month was **~96%** in 2024
- **New customer pipeline**: ~50% of ClassPass subscribers are new to boutique fitness when they join; 80% visit a new studio for the first time through ClassPass
- **Dynamic pricing**: Classes over 80% full with direct members earn **45% higher ClassPass payouts** on average than classes under 50% full

**The Mindbody Acquisition (2021):**
- Mindbody acquired ClassPass + $500M strategic investment from Sixth Street
- Combined entity creates the "most extensive and integrated platform in the industry"
- ClassPass uses Mindbody's API to list studios -- SaaS + marketplace integration
- Combined business growing at 20% year-over-year, expecting $500M in 2024 revenue

**Revenue Guarantee Program:**
- Eligible Mindbody partners are covered by a revenue guarantee: if ClassPass doesn't generate positive incremental revenue within 90 days, the partner is protected
- **99.5% of partners** that opt into the Mindbody Guarantee program gain incremental revenue

**Direct lesson for donedonadone:** ClassPass proves that "filling dead capacity" is an enormously compelling value proposition for venues. donedonadone's pitch to cafes should mirror this: "We fill your empty seats during off-peak hours with guaranteed groups who spend on food and beverage."

### 1.4 How Zomato/Swiggy Made Restaurants Dependent

The Zomato/Swiggy duopoly in India provides a cautionary tale about supply-side dependency -- and lessons on how to create it ethically.

**Dependency Mechanisms:**
- **Commission extraction**: 20-30% of order value, with higher tiers for priority visibility
- **Visibility as currency**: "The only thing publicized is discounts, and if you don't participate, you won't get visibility." Restaurants that pay more get seen more
- **Tiered commission models**: Both platforms introduced priority listing services -- creating a two-tier system where paying more = more orders
- **Data control**: "As dependence on platforms grows, restaurants lose valuable insight into who their customers are and how to build long-term loyalty" -- the platform controls all customer communication

**Revenue extraction pattern:**
- Zomato's food delivery revenue **more than doubled** between Q4 FY23 and Q4 FY25
- Meanwhile, average monthly transacting consumers grew only **26%**
- This means platforms are extracting more money from the same customer base

**Restaurant backlash signals:**
- Middle-tier restaurants caught in a squeeze: too small to renegotiate commissions, too dependent to leave
- Restaurants in some regions have stopped accepting food orders through delivery platforms due to high commissions and "hidden charges"
- After Rapido's disruption, Zomato began sharing customer data with restaurants -- a concession forced by competition

**Lessons for donedonadone (what to do differently):**
1. Keep commissions transparent and reasonable (donedonadone's ₹100-150 per session is a fixed fee, not a percentage -- inherently fairer)
2. Share customer data with venues (build trust, don't hoard information)
3. Create value beyond just sending traffic (tools, analytics, operational support)
4. Never make venues choose between visibility and margin

Based on academic research and marketplace case studies, switching costs for venue partners fall into six categories:

| Switching Cost Type | Description | donedonadone Application |

## 2. The "Venue Operating System" Strategy

### 2.1 Core Philosophy: From Traffic Source to Essential Infrastructure

The most defensible supply-side position in any marketplace is when the platform becomes the **operating system** for its supply partners -- not just a source of demand.

**The progression:**
1. **Level 1 - Traffic Source**: "We send you customers" (easily replaceable)
2. **Level 2 - Booking System**: "We manage your coworking session bookings" (somewhat sticky)
3. **Level 3 - Operating System**: "We run critical parts of your business" (deeply embedded, nearly impossible to replace)

**Evidence from analogous platforms:**

| Platform | Started As | Evolved Into | Switching Cost | Net Revenue Retention |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Toast** | Restaurant POS | Complete restaurant operating system | <3% annual gross churn; **never lost a customer to another cloud-based POS** | 110-135% (customers spend 17%+ more year-over-year) |
| **Mindbody** | Studio scheduling software | Wellness business operating system | "Eliminates the threat of disintermediation" | High (combined $500M revenue with ClassPass) |
| **Shopify** | E-commerce storefront | Complete merchant operating system | Deep ecosystem lock-in (payments, apps, data) | High (Shopify Payments avoids third-party fees) |
| **OpenTable** | Reservation management | Restaurant front-of-house system | "De facto system of record" -- restaurants won't switch | 60,000+ restaurants retained |
| **Instacart** | Grocery delivery | Retail technology platform | POS, catalog, loyalty, media integration | Growing enterprise SaaS revenue |

**Toast's moat is the benchmark:**
- Toast's moat stems primarily from **high switching costs** and an **emerging network effect**
- "Toast's all-in-one system embeds itself into the restaurant's daily operations, creating significant switching costs"
- "Switching costs are not just about data migration but operational risk: restaurants that have experienced seamless uptime are unlikely to gamble on less resilient alternatives"
- Each additional module a customer adds makes the switching cost deeper
- Churn is driven "almost entirely by restaurants going out of business" -- not by competitors

### 2.2 The donedonadone Venue Operating System: Module-by-Module

**What it provides:**
- Real-time foot traffic tracking for coworking sessions

- Comparison to previous periods (week-over-week, month-over-month, year-over-year)

#### Module 2: Inventory Management (Table/Seat Allocation)

**What it provides:**
- Digital floor plan with real-time seat availability

**Technology:**

### 2.3 The SaaS + Marketplace Flywheel Effect

The fundamental insight from OpenTable, Toast, and Mindbody is that **SaaS growth feeds marketplace growth, and marketplace growth feeds SaaS growth**. This flywheel creates exponential growth, lowers customer acquisition costs, and makes the combined platform incredibly difficult to disrupt.

**How this works for donedonadone:**

```
Venue uses analytics dashboard
    --> Venue sees which session types generate most F&B revenue
        --> Venue optimizes their offering for those session types
            --> Better venue experience attracts more coworkers
                --> More coworkers generate more data
                    --> Better data makes analytics more valuable
                        --> Venue is more dependent on the dashboard
                            --> Venue uses more tools (pre-orders, feedback)
                                --> Each tool deepens lock-in
                                    --> [Flywheel accelerates]
```

**Critical principle from Bessemer Venture Partners:** "Without building the SaaS product first and having access to the underlying schedules or locations, there is little lock-in on marketplace models." The SaaS layer is what transforms a substitutable traffic source into an indispensable operating system.

## 4. Exclusive vs Non-Exclusive Partnerships

### 4.1 The Exclusivity Spectrum

Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) identifies three marketplace supply strategies: **comprehensive** (maximum variety), **exclusive** (unique supply unavailable elsewhere), and **curated** (hand-selected quality). For donedonadone, the optimal approach is a tiered exclusivity model.

**When exclusivity works in marketplaces:**
- When exclusive content/supply "meaningfully drives demand" (a16z framework)
- When the market is nascent and competitors haven't entered yet
- When the exclusive partner receives tangible benefits that justify the commitment
- When geographic density matters (exclusive venue in a neighborhood = guaranteed sessions)

**When exclusivity fails:**
- When competitors can simply outspend you to acquire non-exclusive supply (Grubhub learned this with DoorDash)
- When exclusivity creates resentment rather than partnership
- When the exclusive supply isn't differentiated enough to justify limiting options

### 4.2 donedonadone Exclusivity Tiers

#### Tier 1: Time-Slot Exclusive (Recommended for Launch)

**Structure:** Venue is the exclusive donedonadone partner for specific time slots in their micro-zone (e.g., 10 AM-12 PM in a 500m radius)

**What the venue gets:**
- All sessions during that time slot are routed to them
- Priority placement in the app for their neighborhood
- Advanced analytics on session performance
- "Exclusive Partner" badge on their venue profile

**What donedonadone gets:**
- Guaranteed availability during those slots
- Ability to promise users venue availability
- Venue commits to maintaining quality standards during exclusive hours

**Business terms:**
- 6-month initial commitment with auto-renewal
- Venue must maintain a quality score above 4.0/5.0
- Either party can exit with 30-day notice after initial period

### 4.3 ClassPass Exclusivity Lessons

ClassPass's approach to studio exclusivity offers important lessons:

- **No formal exclusivity requirements**: Studios can list on multiple platforms (ClassPass, Gympass, etc.)
- **De facto exclusivity through volume**: Studios that get 28%+ incremental revenue from ClassPass naturally prioritize the platform
- **Revenue guarantee as soft exclusivity**: The 90-day guarantee creates goodwill and reduces risk, making studios less likely to seek alternatives
- **Quality-based differentiation**: Higher-quality studios get higher ClassPass payouts (45% more for popular classes), incentivizing them to stay

**Application to donedonadone:** Rather than forcing exclusivity, make exclusivity attractive through superior benefits. Volume guarantees, better data, and higher venue revenue should make venues want to be exclusive partners.

### 4.4 Legal Frameworks for Venue Partnership Agreements in India

India has **no specific franchise legislation** governing franchise or partnership agreements. Instead, multiple laws apply:

| Law | Relevance |
|---|---|
| Indian Contract Act, 1872 | Foundation for all partnership agreements; requires lawful consideration, free consent, lawful object |
| Competition Act, 2002 | Governs exclusive territory arrangements; anti-competitive agreements can be challenged |
| Trademarks Act, 1999 | Relevant for co-branding and use of donedonadone's brand by partner venues |
| Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 | Preferred dispute resolution mechanism (faster than courts) |
| Information Technology Act, 2000 | Data sharing and privacy provisions for customer data |
| Income Tax Act, 1961 | TDS provisions on commission payments |

**Key legal considerations for donedonadone partnership agreements:**

1. **Non-compete clauses**: Both in-term and post-term non-compete covenants can be enforceable if found "reasonable and not a complete restraint of trade" -- but must be carefully scoped (time-limited, geographically limited)
2. **Exclusivity**: Must specify whether exclusive or non-exclusive, time-bound, and transferable
3. **Territorial rights**: Common to grant territorial exclusivity -- donedonadone can grant "zone exclusive" rights to specific neighborhoods
4. **Termination provisions**: Clear exit terms for both parties; include performance-based termination rights
5. **Data ownership**: Explicitly state that aggregated session data belongs to donedonadone while venue-specific operational data is shared with the venue
6. **IP licensing**: License donedonadone's brand marks to venue for co-branded materials; revocable upon termination

### 4.5 Making Exclusivity Attractive Without Creating Resentment

**The Golden Rule: Exclusive partners should feel privileged, not trapped.**

| Approach | Implementation |
|---|---|
| **Provide more, not restrict more** | Give exclusive partners additional tools, data, and support -- don't just limit their options |
| **Performance-based benefits** | Higher-tier exclusivity unlocks better commission rates, more features, premium placement |
| **Flexible exit** | If donedonadone fails to deliver promised volume, venue can downgrade tier without penalty |
| **Joint success metrics** | Quarterly business reviews focused on venue revenue growth, not just donedonadone metrics |
| **Partner council participation** | Exclusive partners get a seat on the Venue Partner Council to shape platform development |
| **Revenue upside sharing** | As the platform grows, exclusive partners share in the upside through improved terms |

## 5. Venue Quality Standards & Certification

### 5.1 The "donedonadone Certified" Venue Standard

Creating a quality certification program serves a dual purpose: it ensures consistent user experience and creates a supply-side moat as venues invest effort to achieve and maintain certification.

**Quality dimensions and minimum standards:**

| Dimension | Standard | Testing Method |
|---|---|---|
| **WiFi Speed** | Minimum 50 Mbps download, 20 Mbps upload (tested at each table) | Speed test at every seat position during peak and off-peak hours |
| **WiFi Reliability** | <2% packet loss, <50ms latency, supports 20+ simultaneous devices | 4-hour continuous monitoring during certification visit |
| **Power Outlets** | Minimum 1 outlet per 2 seats within arm's reach; USB charging available | Physical count and amperage check at every seating area |
| **Seating Comfort** | Chairs with back support, table height 28-30 inches, minimum 24-inch personal workspace per person | Ergonomic assessment by certification team |
| **Noise Level** | Background noise below 65 dB during session hours (conversation-friendly, not disruptive) | Decibel meter readings at multiple times of day |
| **Lighting** | Minimum 300 lux at desk level; no harsh fluorescent; natural light preferred | Lux meter readings at every coworking seat |
| **Temperature** | Air conditioning maintaining 22-26°C during session hours | Temperature monitoring during certification visit |
| **Restroom Quality** | Clean, accessible, stocked restrooms within 30-second walk | Inspection during certification |
| **Food & Beverage** | Menu available within 10 minutes of ordering; minimum 3 hot beverages, 2 snack options | Menu audit and service time test |
| **Safety & Hygiene** | FSSAI certification displayed, clean tables, fire safety compliance | Document and physical verification |
| **Ambiance** | Pleasant decor, no offensive odors, clean and well-maintained space | Subjective assessment by 2-person certification team |

### 5.2 Airbnb Plus: The Quality Certification Playbook

Airbnb Plus is the most studied example of platform-endorsed quality certification.

**Supply-side moat mechanism:**

4. The certification creates a visible quality gap between Plus and non-Plus

### 5.4 The Certification Process

**Phase 1: Application & Self-Assessment (Week 1)**
1. Venue applies through donedonadone partner portal
2. Completes self-assessment questionnaire (30+ questions covering all quality dimensions)
3. Uploads photos and documentation (FSSAI license, seating layout, WiFi plan)
4. System auto-scores based on self-reported data

**Phase 2: On-Site Certification Visit (Week 2-3)**
1. 2-person certification team visits unannounced during typical operating hours
2. Tests all quality dimensions with calibrated instruments (speed test, decibel meter, lux meter)
3. Conducts mock session: sits for 2 hours as if they were coworkers
4. Evaluates staff friendliness, service speed, and session-readiness
5. Documents findings with photos and measurements

**Phase 3: Scoring & Feedback (Week 3-4)**
1. Venue receives detailed scorecard across all dimensions
2. Overall score: 0-100 (minimum 70 to certify)
3. Specific improvement recommendations for any dimension below threshold
4. Venues scoring 60-69 can re-apply after making improvements (one free re-assessment)

**Phase 4: Certification & Listing (Week 4+)**
1. Certified venues receive "donedonadone Certified" badge
2. Listed on the platform with quality scores visible to users
3. Higher scores = higher ranking in search results
4. Certificate valid for 12 months; annual re-certification required

**Phase 5: Ongoing Monitoring**
1. User feedback continuously updates quality scores
2. If quality score drops below 60 for 30+ consecutive days, venue receives warning
3. If below 60 for 60+ days, certification suspended pending re-assessment
4. Mystery assessments conducted quarterly for Tier 2 and Tier 3 partners

### 8.2 Training Venue Staff on Session Facilitation

**The donedonadone Venue Staff Training Program:**

**Module 1: Understanding Group Coworking (30 minutes)**
- What donedonadone is and why groups come to work
- The group session structure (check-in, work sprints, breaks, checkout)
- How the platform works from the user's perspective
- Why this is different from regular cafe customers

**Module 2: Session Preparation (45 minutes)**
- How to read the session schedule (app notification walkthrough)
- Table preparation checklist: push tables together, ensure power strips accessible, place WiFi cards, clear menu visibility
- Pre-order management: how to receive, prepare, and serve pre-orders before the group arrives
- Special accommodations: allergies, dietary preferences (communicated via app)

**Module 3: During-Session Service (30 minutes)**
- The "invisible support" principle: be available but not intrusive
- When to check in: once after 30 minutes ("Everything okay? Can I get you anything?")
- Handling noise complaints (from other customers about the group, or from the group about the environment)
- Managing table extensions (group wants to stay longer than booked)

**Module 4: Post-Session Protocol (15 minutes)**
- Clearing and resetting tables within 5 minutes
- Encouraging feedback: "How was your coworking session? You can rate us in the app!"
- Handling payment for any walk-up orders not pre-paid
- Flagging any issues to venue manager for donedonadone reporting

**Certification:**
- Staff members who complete all 4 modules receive "donedonadone Trained" designation
- This certification is visible to users booking at the venue (trust signal)
- Annual refresher training required (20 minutes, online)

### 8.4 Uber's Driver Relationship Management: Lessons Learned

**What Uber got right:**
- Created a financial ecosystem (Uber Pro Card, instant pay) that makes the platform central to driver's financial life
- Gamification of ratings and status levels creates intrinsic motivation to maintain standards
- In-app communication tools keep drivers informed without requiring separate channels

**What Uber got wrong:**
- Treated drivers as interchangeable -- high churn (~96% leave within a year) indicates relationship failure
- Subsidize-then-extract model created resentment when payouts were cut
- Limited driver input into platform decisions led to organized protests

**donedonadone's approach (learn from Uber's mistakes):**
1. Treat venues as long-term partners, not interchangeable supply
2. Never subsidize-then-extract: set sustainable commission structure from day one
3. Give venues a voice: Partner Council, feature request system, regular feedback collection
4. Invest in venue success (training, tools, analytics) rather than just extracting fees
5. Create genuine community between venue partners (not just with the platform)

### 10.1 Pop-Up Coworking at Unconventional Venues

**The opportunity:** Pop-up coworking events address the growing demand for novel, experience-driven workspaces. **45% of all coworking spaces in the US** were located in suburban neighborhoods by 2024, showing a massive shift toward non-traditional locations.

### 10.2 The Venue Marketplace

**Phase 3+ feature: A self-service marketplace where new venues can apply to become partners.**

**How it works:**
1. Venue owner visits donedonadone.com/partners
2. Creates a venue profile (photos, location, amenities, capacity, available hours)
3. Completes self-assessment questionnaire
4. System auto-scores and determines eligibility tier
5. If pre-qualified, venue receives certification visit within 2 weeks
6. If certified, venue goes live and starts receiving session bookings

**Benefits of the venue marketplace:**
- Scales venue acquisition beyond the founding team's capacity
- Creates a pipeline of interested venues for geographic expansion
- Enables data-driven venue selection (areas with high user demand but low supply)
- Reduces per-venue acquisition cost as the system becomes more efficient

**Quality gates:**
- Auto-screening filters: must have WiFi, power outlets, seating for 4+, F&B service
- Self-assessment score must be 60+ to schedule certification
- Two-week trial period after certification (can be removed if quality drops below threshold)
- User feedback rapidly validates or invalidates new venue quality

### 10.3 Revenue-Sharing Innovations

#### Model 1: Performance-Based Pricing

**Structure:** donedonadone's commission scales with the value delivered to the venue.

| Sessions/Month | Commission per Session | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1-20 | ₹0 (free trial period) | Build trust, prove value |
| 21-50 | ₹25 per session | Low commitment, scaling partnership |
| 51-100 | ₹50 per session | Meaningful volume, venue sees clear ROI |
| 101+ | ₹75 per session | High volume, deep partnership |

**Alternative: Revenue share on pre-orders**
- 90% to venue / 10% to donedonadone on all pre-order F&B sales
- This aligns incentives: donedonadone benefits when venues offer better menus and coworkers order more
- More palatable than a flat fee for smaller venues

#### Model 2: Minimum Guarantee

**Structure:** donedonadone guarantees a minimum number of sessions per month to exclusive partners.

| Partner Tier | Minimum Guarantee | If Not Met |
|---|---|---|
| Zone Exclusive | 40 sessions/month | donedonadone pays venue ₹100 per missed session |
| Flagship | 80 sessions/month | donedonadone pays venue ₹150 per missed session |

**Why this works:**
- Eliminates venue's downside risk: "If we don't send enough groups, we pay you"
- Mirrors ClassPass's revenue guarantee (99.5% of partners gained incremental revenue)
- Creates confidence for venues to invest in donedonadone-specific infrastructure
- Only viable once platform has sufficient demand -- use in Phase 2+

#### Model 3: Hybrid Retainer + Performance

**Structure:** Small monthly retainer guarantees venue availability + performance bonuses for quality.

| Component | Amount | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly retainer (venue receives) | ₹5,000/month | For maintaining donedonadone-ready status during agreed hours |
| Quality bonus | ₹2,000-5,000/month | If donedonadone Score stays above 85 |
| Volume bonus | ₹50 per session above 30 sessions/month | Incentivizes venues to optimize for more sessions |

## Summary: The Supply-Side Moat Thesis

donedonadone's venue partnership strategy is designed to create a supply-side moat that deepens automatically over time through six reinforcing mechanisms:

1. **Venue Operating System** (Toast model): Become indispensable infrastructure, not just a traffic source. Every tool a venue adopts increases switching costs.

2. **Data Accumulation** (Instacart model): Historical performance data, competitive benchmarks, and predictive analytics become more valuable over time and cannot be transferred to a competitor.

3. **Quality Certification** (Airbnb Plus model): Venues invest effort to earn and maintain certification. The "donedonadone Certified" badge becomes a recognized quality signal that has no equivalent elsewhere.

4. **Exclusive Partnerships** (a16z framework): Tiered exclusivity creates geographic supply advantages that competitors cannot easily replicate.

5. **Revenue Dependency** (ClassPass model): As donedonadone grows to represent 15-30% of a venue's revenue, the switching cost becomes existential.

6. **Community Lock-In** (Network effects): Venue partner community, Champion relationships, and peer benchmarking create social switching costs beyond financial ones.

**The end state:** A venue partner that has been on donedonadone for 2+ years has accumulated irreplaceable data, trained their staff on the platform's tools, organized their operations around the session schedule, built their reputation through quality scores and reviews, and derives 15-30% of their revenue from the platform. The cost of leaving is not just financial -- it's operational, reputational, and relational.

**This is how you build a supply-side moat that competitors cannot replicate with capital alone.**
