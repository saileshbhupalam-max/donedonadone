## 1. How Top Platforms Expanded Geographically

### 1.1 Uber: The Gold Standard City Launch Playbook

**Overview:** Uber scaled from a single city (San Francisco) to 450+ markets globally, becoming the definitive case study in geographic marketplace expansion.

**The City Launcher Role:**
- Employee #99 Scott Gorlick built Uber in Atlanta and helped scale from 10 cities to $10B in revenue
- Each city had a dedicated "City Launcher" -- a local hire with deep market knowledge and capacity for long hours
- The launch team consisted of 4 key roles: Launcher, General Manager, Operations Manager, Marketing Manager
- City Launchers were effectively "startup founders" but with corporate backing and a proven playbook

**The 180-Step Launch Playbook:**

| Phase | Key Activities | Duration |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| Pre-Launch | Market research, regulatory assessment, driver recruitment | 4-6 weeks |
| Soft Launch | 20-30 drivers onboarded, invite-only rider access | 1-2 weeks |
| Public Launch | Marketing blitz, press coverage, event partnerships | 1 week |
| Growth | Incentive programs, geographic expansion within city | 4-8 weeks |
| Optimization | Unit economics focus, driver retention, coverage density | Ongoing |

**Key Metrics Tracked Weekly Per City:**
1. Gross bookings (total revenue generated)
2. Trips completed (transaction volume)
3. Drivers onboarded (supply growth)
4. Driver retention: 28-day, 56-day, 96-day periods
5. Good retention benchmark: 25-30% of drivers retained after 1 year
6. Rider retention: total spend, rides per week, rides per month
7. Average ETA (supply density indicator)

**How They Achieved Density Quickly:**
- Identified the geographic core where density would be easiest (downtown business district, nightlife areas)
- Made it risk-free for drivers: hourly earning guarantees, sign-up rewards, referral rewards
- Needed only 20-30 drivers before a city launch for sufficient supply
- Started with a narrow geographic zone, then expanded outward
- Treated each city like a startup but with financial backing of US headquarters

**Playbook Evolution:**
- Each launch included mistakes that were learned from and incorporated back
- Living document continuously updated -- grew from initial version to 180+ steps
- By later launches, had "plug-and-play" strategy down to minute details
- Core learnings from each market applied to update the master playbook

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- The 20-30 driver minimum maps to our need for 5-10 partner venues before neighborhood launch
- City Launcher role maps to our "Neighborhood Lead" -- local person who knows the area
- Narrow geographic start then expand outward mirrors our neighborhood-first approach
- Weekly metric tracking is essential for rapid iteration

### 1.2 Airbnb: Supply-First Strategy and Localization

**Overview:** Airbnb expanded to 190+ markets with 7+ million listings, becoming available in 26 languages with 32 currencies for guests and 65 currencies for host payouts.

**The Photographer Program:**
- Deployed 1,000+ photographers globally to shoot host properties professionally
- Covered 13,000 properties in 383 cities across 6 continents
- Properties with professional photos were booked 2.5x more frequently
- Professional photos generated an average of $1,025/month more revenue per listing
- Program solved both quality and trust problems simultaneously
- **Key insight:** Investing in supply quality drives demand organically

**The "Snow White" Storyboarding Project:**
- CEO Brian Chesky borrowed Disney's storyboarding methodology from a Walt Disney biography
- Hired Pixar animator Nick Sung to create storyboards of the complete user journey
- Created 3 storyboard narratives: host process, guest process, hiring process
- Listed every emotional moment in an Airbnb stay and designed for each
- This customer-journey-first approach guided all product and expansion decisions

**Supply-First Expansion Strategy:**
- Always secured supply before marketing to demand
- In new markets: focused on host acquisition through local marketing, product customization, and outreach
- Key markets like Japan, Germany, Brazil, Mexico each received tailored growth strategies
- Opened regional hubs to customize strategies per local market (Kenya, Peru, Vietnam)
- Listings showed local language, customs, and pricing for domestic travelers

**Localization Approach:**
- Created a product without location-specific elements first (location-neutral base)
- Then meticulously localized for each market
- Built an internationalization platform with local stores that cached translations on-device
- Used machine translation plus language detection for content localization
- Payment localization: 32 booking currencies, 65 payout currencies

**Ambassador Program:**
- Ambassadors must be Superhosts with accounts in good standing
- Earn cash bonuses for each new host recruited who completes a qualifying reservation
- New hosts must complete their first booking within 150 days, with reservation value exceeding $100
- Referrals also receive a one-time cash reward (~10% of ambassador earnings)
- Ambassadors reviewed quarterly: must complete at least 1 referral every 3 months
- Primary role: mentor first-time hosts through setup process

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- Photographer program = our venue photography/videography investment (show the cafe vibe)
- Snow White storyboarding = map every emotional touchpoint of a group coworking session
- Supply-first = secure 5-10 great venues before marketing to coworkers
- Ambassador model = recruit power coworkers who bring their network and mentor new users

### 1.3 ClassPass: Market-by-Market Fitness Marketplace

**Overview:** ClassPass expanded from 2 cities to 35+ cities and eventually 30,000+ partner studios with 1,000+ corporate partners.

**Studio Onboarding Strategy:**
- Aggressively courted the top studios in each new city first
- Offered more generous per-class rates to premium studios
- Provided sign-up bonuses worth thousands of dollars for exclusivity
- Once top studios joined, tier 2 and tier 3 studios followed naturally
- The "anchor tenant" strategy created a quality halo effect

**Demand Aggregation Approach:**
- Special low-priced trial offers for first month in new markets
- Significant spend on Google Search Ads and Facebook Ads for local acquisition
- Localized marketing efforts and partnerships with local studios
- Focused on urban centers with strong demand for flexible fitness options

**Expansion Phases:**
| Phase | Cities | Strategy |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Launch (2013-2014) | 2 cities | NYC and a few others, product-market fit |
| Rapid Growth (2014-2015) | 2-35 cities | Aggressive studio onboarding, trial pricing |
| Category Expansion | 35+ cities | Added wellness, beauty, beyond fitness |
| International | 28+ countries | 30,000 studios globally |

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- "Anchor tenant" strategy = sign the best, most Instagrammable cafes in each neighborhood first
- Trial pricing = offer first session free or at deep discount
- Top-down supply acquisition creates quality perception that attracts both more venues and users

### 1.4 Grab & Gojek: Super App Expansion in Southeast Asia

**Grab's Regional-First Approach:**
- Founded 2012 in Malaysia as a taxi app
- Expanded to Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, Vietnam, Philippines, Myanmar, Cambodia
- Acquired Uber's Southeast Asia operations in 2018 -- leapfrogged in one move
- Focused on "horizontal expansion" through strategic acquisitions and fintech infrastructure
- Preferred partnerships and joint ventures for faster market entry
- GrabFood launched May 2018, reached 200+ cities across SEA

**Gojek's Depth-First Approach:**
- Founded 2010 in Indonesia as motorcycle ride-hailing call center
- Launched app in 2015
- Operates in Indonesia (90% of city operations), Thailand, Vietnam, Singapore
- Focused on "vertical depth" -- dominating Indonesia before expanding
- Used acquisitions for tighter control of home market
- Indonesia accounts for ~66% of Grab's cities but ~90% of Gojek's

**Strategic Contrast -- Breadth vs Depth:**

| Dimension | Grab | Gojek |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Countries | 8 | 4 |
| Primary Strategy | Horizontal breadth | Vertical depth |
| Expansion Method | Partnerships, JVs | Acquisitions |
| Market Focus | Regional dominance | Home market dominance |
| Services | Transport-first | Multi-service from start |

**Key Lessons for Emerging Markets:**
- Local understanding trumps global brand in SEA/India
- Cash payments and local payment methods are essential (not just credit cards)
- Motorcycle/two-wheeler culture matters in city operations
- Regulatory relationships are critical in every market
- Super app strategy: solve one problem well, then expand services

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- Gojek's depth-first strategy is more applicable -- dominate Bangalore before expanding
- Local payment integration (UPI) is essential for India
- Build deep in one market rather than spreading thin across many

### 1.5 OYO: India's Cautionary Tale of Hyper-Expansion

**The Rise (2013-2019):**
- Founded by Ritesh Agarwal in 2013
- Grew to 800 cities in 80 countries by 2019
- Revenue increased 4.5x to Rs 63.3 billion
- Became one of the world's largest hotel chains by room count

**What Went Wrong:**

| Problem | Detail | Impact |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Speed over depth | 80 countries in ~5 years | No operational excellence |
| Minimum guarantee model | Guaranteed income to hotels | Legal disputes when occupancy fell short |
| Quality collapse | 200+ hotel owners ended agreements | Reputation damage, customer complaints |
| Asset-heavy pivot | Shifted from franchising to leasing | Ballooning operational liabilities |
| Losses spiraled | Losses widened 6.4x to Rs 23.65 billion (2019) | Financial unsustainability |
| Cultural mismatch | Uniform approach globally | Failed to adapt to local markets |

**What Went Right (Post-Correction):**
- Repositioned as "Shopify for Hotels" -- selling software, tools, and brand access
- Shifted from taking on operational burdens to enabling hotel owners
- Reduced from 80 countries to focus markets
- Moved toward sustainable unit economics per property

**Key Lessons for donedonadone:**
1. **Never sacrifice quality for speed** -- better to have 10 excellent venues than 50 mediocre ones
2. **Don't guarantee income to partners** -- use revenue share, not minimum guarantees
3. **Stay asset-light** -- we don't lease cafe space, we partner with existing cafes
4. **Local adaptation is non-negotiable** -- each city/neighborhood has unique characteristics
5. **Unit economics must work before expanding** -- prove the model in HSR Layout first
6. **Platform model > operations model** -- enable venues, don't operate them

### 1.6 Zomato & Swiggy: India's Hyperlocal Marketplace Masters

**Zomato's Expansion Timeline:**

| Year | Milestone | Cities |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 2008 | Founded as Foodiebay | 1 (Delhi) |
| 2011 | Expanded listings | 25+ |
| 2012 | Restaurant reviews nationwide | 50+ |
| 2015 | Launched food delivery | 100+ |
| 2020 | Acquired UberEats India | 500+ |
| 2024 | Full delivery + Blinkit | 1,000+ |

**Zomato's Expansion Strategy:**
- Started as listings/reviews (zero marginal cost per city) before delivery
- Used acquisitions to leapfrog (UberEats India)
- 80% of dark stores concentrated in top 8 cities
- Hyperlocal data: delivery hub placement, data analytics for delivery optimization
- Created distinct brands for different services (Blinkit for grocery)

**Swiggy's Expansion Strategy:**
- Started 2015 in Bangalore as hyperlocal logistics platform
- Built food delivery that overtook Zomato's topline in 5 years
- Now operates Bolt (quick food delivery) in 500+ cities
- Instamart expanded to 100 cities for quick commerce

**Swiggy's Tier 2/3 City Launch Playbook:**
- 1 in 4 new users come from tier 2/3 cities (2025)
- Partnered with local brands and vendors for regional customization
- Example: Mangalore gets Narans Foods and Ideal Icecream; Bhopal gets Top N Town Breads
- Patna became fastest city to reach 1,000+ daily orders (4 days after launch)
- Raipur was first city to hit 300 orders on launch day
- Introduced "megapods" (10,000-12,000 sq ft dark stores) for 50,000 SKUs

**Key Insights for donedonadone:**
- Start with low-cost, zero-marginal-cost services (discovery/listings) before high-ops services
- Hyperlocal data collection from day 1 informs expansion decisions
- Regional customization drives adoption in new markets
- Tier 2/3 cities are hungry for services -- don't assume metros only
- Speed to first milestone (1,000 orders) indicates market readiness

### 1.7 Cult.fit: Multi-City Fitness Marketplace in India

**Expansion Model (3-Pronged):**
1. **Company-owned centers** -- full control, higher cost
2. **Marketplace model** -- onboard existing gyms (low cost, fast)
3. **Franchise system** -- local entrepreneurs operate under brand

**Growth Trajectory:**
- Started in Bangalore
- Now present in 130+ cities with 580+ gyms across 50+ cities
- Acquired Gold's Gym franchise rights in 2021: instant access to 140+ centers in 90+ cities
- Revenue: Rs 670 crore from fitness subscriptions (72.3% of total), up 46.6% YoY

**Tier 2 Strategy:**
- Franchise-led expansion in smaller cities
- Partner with local entrepreneurs who understand regional markets
- Premium fitness positioned as aspirational in tier 2 markets
- Hybrid model (offline + online) allows users to switch between in-person and virtual

**Key Lessons:**
- Acquisition (Gold's Gym) provided instant geographic scale without city-by-city grind
- Franchise model reduces capital requirements for expansion
- Hybrid online/offline model builds demand before physical presence
- Local entrepreneurs as franchise partners bring market knowledge and investment

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- Marketplace model for venues (list existing cafes) + franchise model for expansion
- Consider acquiring or partnering with existing coworking aggregators (GoFloaters, etc.)
- Online community building can precede physical venue partnerships in new cities
- Local franchise partners could be community leads who manage their neighborhood

### 1.8 WeWork: Lessons from Expansion Failure

**What Failed:**
- Growth-at-all-cost model: opened lavish locations before existing ones profitable
- $3 billion cumulative losses by failed 2019 IPO
- Long-term lease commitments (15-year leases) with short-term revenue (month-to-month members)
- Liability mismatch: ~$47 billion in lease obligations
- Diversification without core profitability (WeLive, Rise by We, WeGrow school)
- Founder-driven decisions without governance checks
- Filed for bankruptcy in November 2023

**Post-WeWork Industry Shift:**
- Operators now favor management agreements over traditional leases
- Focus on profitability per location rather than rapid expansion
- Revenue-share models with building owners instead of long-term leases
- Community and experience over flashy real estate

**Anti-Patterns for donedonadone:**
1. **Never sign long-term venue commitments** -- partnership agreements with easy exit
2. **Each venue must have positive unit economics before adding more**
3. **Don't diversify before core model works** -- stay focused on group coworking
4. **Governance and financial discipline from day 1**
5. **Asset-light always** -- we use venues we don't own
6. **Real community > expensive real estate**

### 1.9 Nextdoor: Neighborhood-by-Neighborhood Growth

**Core Philosophy:** "Make Nextdoor as amazing as possible for a particular group of people and expand from there."

**Expansion Methodology:**
- Users define their own neighborhood boundaries
- Users invite their own neighbors (viral loop)
- Focus on depth in one neighborhood before breadth across many
- 400% increase in active neighborhoods within one year (reached 22,500 by Oct 2013)

**Scalable Invitation Model:**
- Integrated with Gmail, iCloud, LinkedIn for contact importing
- Simplified invitation process to increase volume and frequency
- Each neighborhood needed a "founding member" who verified the area
- Address verification (postcard with PIN) ensured real local residents

**Key Insights:**
- Hyperlocal density matters more than broad coverage
- User-driven expansion (invitations) is cheaper and more authentic than marketing
- Neighborhood boundaries should be user-defined, not arbitrarily imposed
- Quality of the initial community determines expansion success

**Relevance to donedonadone:**
- Let early users in each neighborhood invite their coworking friends
- Define "neighborhoods" based on natural boundaries (walking distance to venues)
- A single power user can seed an entire neighborhood
- Address/workplace verification adds trust

## 2. The Expansion Playbook Template

### 2.1 Overview: The donedonadone Launch Process

Based on analysis of 10+ platform expansion case studies, here is the definitive playbook for launching donedonadone in any new neighborhood, city, or country.

**Expansion Unit Hierarchy:**
```
Country → City → Zone (cluster of neighborhoods) → Neighborhood (primary unit)
```

**The neighborhood is our atomic unit of expansion** -- analogous to Uber's city. We achieve density at the neighborhood level before expanding to adjacent neighborhoods, then zones, then cities.

### 2.2 Phase 1: Pre-Launch (Weeks -6 to -1)

#### A. Market Assessment Criteria

**Quantitative Scoring (100-point scale):**

| Criterion | Weight | Data Source | Scoring |
|-----------|--------|-------------|---------|
| Cafe/coworking density | 20 pts | Google Maps, Zomato | 15+ venues = 20pts, 10-14 = 15pts, 5-9 = 10pts |
| Remote worker population | 20 pts | LinkedIn data, startup density | High = 20, Medium = 15, Low = 10 |
| Avg income level | 15 pts | Census, real estate prices | Matches tier = 15, Slightly off = 10 |
| Public transit access | 10 pts | Metro/bus connectivity | Metro access = 10, Bus = 7, Poor = 3 |
| Competitor presence | 10 pts | Market scan | None = 10, Weak = 7, Strong = 3 |
| Cafe WiFi quality | 10 pts | Speed test surveys | 50+ Mbps = 10, 25-50 = 7, <25 = 3 |
| Age demographics (22-40) | 10 pts | Census data | 40%+ young professionals = 10 |
| Walking/commute radius | 5 pts | Google Maps | 2km radius has 5+ venues = 5 |

**Minimum score to proceed: 65/100**

**Qualitative Assessment:**
- Visit 10+ cafes in the neighborhood personally
- Talk to 20+ remote workers/freelancers in the area
- Assess "vibe" -- is this a neighborhood where people already work from cafes?
- Check Instagram/social media for cafe work culture signals
- Identify local community groups (WhatsApp, Telegram, Facebook)
- Assess safety, especially for women working late

**HSR Layout Benchmark (Reference Score: 88/100):**
- Cafe density: 20/20 (4.8% of Bangalore's total restaurants -- highest concentration)
- Remote worker population: 20/20 (50+ registered startups, hundreds of freelancers)
- Income level: 15/15 (prime South Bangalore)
- Transit: 7/10 (bus access, metro coming)
- Competitors: 8/10 (GoFloaters present but no group coworking)
- WiFi: 8/10 (most cafes have decent WiFi)
- Demographics: 10/10 (young professional concentration)
- Walking radius: 5/5 (Sector 7 has excellent venue density)

### 2.4 Phase 3: Traction (Months 2-3)

#### A. When to Add More Venues

**Venue Expansion Triggers:**

| Signal | Threshold | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Existing venues >70% utilized | 3 consecutive weeks | Add 2-3 new venues |
| Users requesting specific area | 5+ requests for same area | Scout that micro-area |
| Session waitlists forming | 3+ sessions with waitlists | Add parallel sessions at new venues |
| New venue inbound interest | Quality venue approaches us | Fast-track onboarding |
| Adjacent neighborhood demand | 10+ users from neighboring area | Expand geographic boundary |

**Venue Capacity Formula:**
```
Venues needed = (Weekly active users x Sessions per user per week) /
                (Average seats per session x Sessions per venue per week x Utilization target)

Example: (100 users x 2 sessions) / (4 seats x 5 sessions x 0.7 utilization) = 14.3 → 15 venues needed
```

#### B. Local Product-Market Fit Indicators

**PMF at the neighborhood level is achieved when:**

| Indicator | PMF Threshold | Measurement |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| 30-day retention | >40% | Users who book again within 30 days |
| Organic growth rate | >30% of new users via referral | Referral tracking |
| NPS | >50 | Monthly survey |
| Session fill rate | >60% | Average across all sessions |
| Revenue per user per month | >Rs 400 | Average across active users |
| Venue retention | >80% | Venues still active after 3 months |
| Word-of-mouth ratio | >3:1 | Organic:paid user acquisition |
| User-initiated sessions | Users requesting specific times/venues | Qualitative signal |

**The "Magic Number" for Neighborhood PMF:**
A neighborhood has achieved PMF when it sustains **50+ active weekly users** across **5+ venues** with **40%+ 30-day retention** for **4 consecutive weeks**.

### 5.5 Regulatory Considerations by Region

**Key Compliance Actions:**

3. Payment compliance: PCI-DSS for card data, local payment processor partnerships
4. Right to deletion: Users must be able to delete their data
5. Cross-border data transfer: Establish legal mechanisms (standard contractual clauses)

- Lifetime value of ambassador-referred users is 2-3x higher than paid acquisition
- Break-even: Ambassador pays for themselves when their users generate Rs 15,000-20,000 in platform fees
- At Rs 100/session and 2 sessions/user/month, need 75-100 active users per ambassador to break even
- Timeline to break-even: typically 3-4 months

### 7.3 Minimum Viable Density (MVD)

**Definition:** The minimum number of users and venues within a geographic area needed for the marketplace to function and deliver value.

**MVD Calculation for donedonadone:**

| Component | Minimum | Ideal | Formula Basis |
|-----------|---------|-------|---------------|
| Venues per neighborhood | 5 | 10+ | Need variety for matching |
| Active weekly users | 25 | 50+ | Need enough for diverse groups |
| Sessions per week | 10 | 20+ | 2 sessions/venue minimum |
| Group fill rate | 60% | 80%+ | 3+ out of 5 seats filled |
| Geographic radius | 3 km | 2 km | Walking/short commute distance |

**The Density Flywheel:**
```
More venues → More session variety → More users attracted
    ↑                                         ↓
Better matching ← More data ← More sessions completed
```

**When density is TOO LOW (warning signs):**
- Sessions regularly have only 2 people (minimum should be 3)
- Users travel >20 minutes to reach a venue
- Same users matched repeatedly (not enough variety)
- Venues report empty reserved tables
- Users complain about limited time/venue options

**When density is SUFFICIENT:**
- Average group size: 4+ people
- Users have 3+ venue options within 10-minute travel
- New users matched with new people each session
- Wait times for desired sessions <24 hours
- Organic word-of-mouth accelerating

## 8. Technology Architecture for Multi-Geography

### 8.1 Multi-Tenant vs Multi-Instance

**Recommendation: Multi-Tenant Architecture**

| Aspect | Multi-Tenant (Recommended) | Multi-Instance | Rationale |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------|-----------|
| Database | Single database, row-level isolation | Separate databases per city/country | Cost-effective, easier to maintain |
| Codebase | Single codebase | Separate deployments | Faster feature rollouts to all markets |
| Data isolation | Row-level security (Supabase RLS) | Physical separation | RLS provides sufficient isolation |
| Scalability | Horizontal scaling | Scale each instance | More efficient resource utilization |
| Cost | Lower | Higher | Shared infrastructure |
| Compliance | Can handle most requirements | Needed for strict data localization | Use multi-region for data residency |
| Deployment | One deployment | N deployments | Simpler operations |

**When to switch to Multi-Instance:**
- Regulatory requirement for complete data isolation (e.g., China, Russia)
- Market-specific customization exceeds 30% of codebase
- Acquisition of local platform with existing tech stack
- Performance requirements demand geographic proximity (latency <50ms)

### 8.2 Database Schema for Multi-Geography

**Key Schema Extensions (Supabase/PostgreSQL):**

```sql

CREATE TABLE countries (

    THB: { code: 'THB', symbol: '฿', symbolPosition: 'before', decimalPlaces: 2, thousandsSeparator: ',', decimalSeparator: '.' },
    USD: { code: 'USD', symbol: '$', symbolPosition: 'before', decimalPlaces: 2, thousandsSeparator: ',', decimalSeparator: '.' },

```

**Timezone Handling (following Uber's approach):**
- Store all times in UTC in the database
- Convert to local timezone only on client display
- Use IANA timezone database (e.g., 'Asia/Kolkata', 'Asia/Jakarta')
- Session times displayed in venue's local timezone
- User notifications in user's local timezone
- Cross-timezone bookings handled gracefully (user in Mumbai booking Bangalore session)

**Best Practices:**
- Never store local times in the database
- Always include timezone in API responses
- Use libraries like `date-fns-tz` or `luxon` for timezone operations
- Test edge cases: DST transitions (for international markets), IST +5:30 half-hour offset

## Summary: The donedonadone Expansion Principles

### Ten Commandments of Geographic Expansion

1. **Depth before breadth** -- Dominate HSR Layout completely before expanding to Koramangala
2. **Supply before demand** -- Secure 5-10 great venues before marketing to users
3. **Quality before speed** -- 10 excellent venues > 50 mediocre ones (OYO lesson)
4. **Asset-light always** -- Partner with venues, never lease or own (WeWork lesson)
5. **Data-driven decisions** -- 65/100 assessment score minimum, no gut-feel launches
6. **Local leadership** -- Every neighborhood needs someone who lives and breathes it
7. **Playbook iteration** -- Each launch updates the playbook for the next one (Uber model)
8. **Community first** -- Build the community, and the marketplace will follow (Nextdoor model)
9. **Unit economics must work** -- Prove the model works in one place before scaling it everywhere
10. **Technology enables, people execute** -- Build tools that make each launch faster, but never forget that local relationships drive success

### The North Star Metric for Expansion

**1,000 bookings/day across all active neighborhoods**

This breaks down to:
- 10 neighborhoods x 100 bookings/day each, OR
- 25 neighborhoods x 40 bookings/day each, OR
- 50 neighborhoods x 20 bookings/day each

The most capital-efficient path is **25 high-density neighborhoods across 3-5 cities**, each operating at full capacity with strong community effects.
